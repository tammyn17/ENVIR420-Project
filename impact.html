Environmental Impact

Cannabis Supply Chain

The cannabis supply chain consists of five main stages: Cultivation, Manufacturing, Distribution, Transportation, and Retail.

Track and Trace systems are used throughout the supply chain for compliance.

Cultivation

Cultivation involves six stages:

  • Germination
  • Seeding
  • Vegetative
  • Pre-flowering
  • Flowering
  • Harvesting

Post-harvest processes include drying, curing, and trimming.

Manufacturing

Manufacturing involves several steps:

  • Extraction (solvent and non-solvent methods)
  • Light manufacturing (packaging, pre-rolls)
  • Infusion (for edibles)

Distribution

Distribution challenges include:

  • State-specific regulations
  • Restrictions on vehicle size
  • Prohibition of interstate transport
  • Intra-state transport regulations

Transportation

Transportation requires specific licenses and is heavily regulated. Testing is crucial for compliance and includes:

  • Contamination checks
  • Pesticide screening
  • Potency testing
  • Terpene profiling

Quality Assurance ensures proper packaging and labeling.

Retail

Retail (dispensaries) face challenges such as:

  • Product sampling and knowledge
  • Compliance concerns
  • Finding vendors (state-restricted)
  • Competition from the black market

The industry is heavily regulated, with serious consequences for non-compliance.

Environmental Concerns in Cannabis Production

Key environmental concerns in cannabis production include:

  • High energy use for indoor grows (lighting, heating/cooling, dehumidifying, ventilation)
  • Water intensity, especially in drier environments
  • Pesticide misuse and overuse, which pose risks to workers, consumers, and the environment

Policy Recommendations

Land Use

Better planning of cultivation sites to minimize negative environmental impacts, including:

  • Land use planning strategies to regulate the location and size of new farming areas
  • Relocating existing farms onto more suitable land
  • Implementing wildlife-conscious farming practices to prevent negative impacts on wildlife
  • Urban zoning policies to ensure vulnerable communities are not disproportionately affected

Water Use

Effective water management is crucial, especially since large amounts of cannabis are grown in semi-arid/drought-prone environments. Strategies include:

  • Establishing water licensing systems
  • Incentivizing efficient water usage
  • Using rainwater storage, automated irrigation, and water treatment and reuse strategies

Pesticide Use

To address pesticide use, policy changes should include:

  • Developing rigorous testing standards for contaminants in cannabis products
  • Certifying and educating producers on alternative pest control methods
  • Incentivizing producers to avoid illegal chemicals
  • Implementing thorough supply chain tracing and environmental monitoring to prevent sourcing from illegal markets
  • Encouraging the use of biologically derived pesticide products and integrated pest management methods

Energy Use

To manage energy use, regulations should require:

  • Growers to submit energy plans that comply with existing standards
  • Reports on energy usage

Best practices include prioritizing energy-efficient light sources, renewable energy, and scheduling operations around peak demand times.

Air Pollution

To reduce air pollution, policies should focus on:

  • Issuing permits based on ozone-formation simulations
  • Ensuring transparency regarding BVOCs (Biogenic Volatile Organic Compounds) and masking agents related to human health in cultivation facilities
  • Utilizing carbon filtration and closed-loop cannabis extraction systems
